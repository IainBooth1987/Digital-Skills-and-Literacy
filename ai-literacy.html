Module 01

From how models learn to ethical responsibilities — a grounded introduction to AI for educators and support staff.

Foundations

What is AI and how does it work?

Definition

Artificial Intelligence

Computer systems designed to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence — understanding language, recognising patterns, making decisions. AI is an umbrella term covering many techniques, from rule-based logic to modern neural networks.

The dominant approach

Machine Learning

Rather than being explicitly programmed with rules, machine learning systems learn patterns from data. Show the system enough examples and it adjusts its internal parameters until it can generalise. This underlies virtually every AI tool staff will encounter today.

Key Distinction AI is the broad field; machine learning is the most widely used approach within it. When a colleague refers to "AI", they almost certainly mean a machine learning system — and specifically, in most workplace contexts, a large language model.

Comparison

Rule-Based vs Machine Learning Systems

Rule-Based Systems Machine Learning Systems
Follow explicit if/then rules written by programmers Learn statistical patterns from large amounts of training data
Predictable and consistent — same input, same output Can handle ambiguity and nuance; outputs may vary
Brittle — fail completely when inputs fall outside defined rules Can generalise to inputs never seen during training
Transparent — you can inspect and audit every rule Often a "black box" — internal reasoning is difficult to interpret
Example: An autocorrect system that replaces specific known misspellings Example: Microsoft Copilot understanding the context and tone of an email

Process

How a model is trained

1

Data Collection

Enormous volumes of text, images, or other data are gathered. GPT-4, the model behind many AI tools, is estimated to have been trained on over a trillion words drawn from books, websites, academic papers, and code repositories.

2

Training

The model processes this data repeatedly, adjusting billions of internal numerical weights to reduce its prediction errors. For a language model, the core task is simple: predict the next word. Repeated at massive scale, this produces remarkably capable systems.

3

Fine-Tuning with Human Feedback

Human reviewers rate the model's outputs, and those ratings are used to further adjust the model — steering it towards helpful, accurate, and safe responses. This step is what makes a raw language model into a useful assistant.

4

Deployment

The finished model is packaged into a product — Copilot, ChatGPT, Gemini. Once deployed, the model itself is frozen. It does not learn from your conversations unless Anthropic, Microsoft, or OpenAI explicitly retrain it on new data.

Knowledge Cut-Off Date A deployed model knows nothing about events after its training ended. GPT-4's training data cuts off in early 2024; Copilot uses web search to partially compensate. Always verify current policy, legislation, or news from authoritative sources — never rely on an AI tool alone.

Glossary

Six terms worth knowing

Term

Algorithm

The set of mathematical operations a model uses to transform an input (your prompt) into an output (a response). Not a single rule — a complex chain of matrix calculations.

Term

Model

The trained system itself — billions of numerical weights that encode the patterns learned from training data. GPT-4, Copilot, and Gemini are all different models.

Term

Dataset

The collection of examples used to train the model. Its size, diversity, and quality directly determine what the model can and cannot do reliably.

Term

Parameters

The individual numerical values adjusted during training. GPT-4 has an estimated one trillion parameters. More parameters generally — but not always — means greater capability.

Term

Inference

The process of using a trained model to generate a response. Every time you send a message to Copilot or ChatGPT, the model runs an inference step.

Term

Prompt

The input you provide to an AI model — a question, instruction, or context. The quality and specificity of the prompt is the single biggest factor in the quality of the output.

Why this matters for staff Any content shared under your professional name is your responsibility — regardless of whether AI produced a first draft. A hallucinated statistic in a parent communication or a fabricated source in a departmental report reflects on you, not the tool.

The Core Problem

What is a hallucination?

AI language models generate text by predicting the most statistically plausible next token — they do not retrieve facts from a verified database. When a model lacks reliable training data on a topic, it fills the gap with plausible-sounding fabrications. These are called hallucinations: confident, fluent, detailed — and wrong.

Real-World Example — Fabricated Legal Citations
What ChatGPT produced

In 2023, New York lawyer Steven Schwartz submitted a legal brief citing six court cases provided by ChatGPT in support of his client's case. The citations were detailed and convincing — case names, judges, rulings, legal arguments. A judge ordered Schwartz to explain himself.

The reality

None of the six cases existed. Every citation had been invented by the model. Schwartz faced disciplinary proceedings and a fine. The incident became one of the most widely reported demonstrations of the real professional consequences of unchecked AI output.

Real-World Example — Fabricated Academic Research
The pattern

Researchers who tested multiple large language models by asking for academic references on specialist topics found that a significant proportion of the citations returned were either entirely invented — wrong author, wrong journal, wrong year — or real papers whose conclusions had been misrepresented.

The lesson for staff

If an AI tool produces a named study, a statistic, or a quotation, treat it as a lead to verify — not a source to use. Always locate and read the original document before citing it in any professional context.

Fact-Checking

A practical verification process

1

Identify every specific claim

Names, dates, statistics, quotations, case studies, and citations are the highest-risk elements. Flag each one before you publish or share.

2

Go to primary sources

Search for the original document, organisation website, or official record directly. Do not use a second AI tool to verify the first — they share many of the same training data and errors.

3

Check the date

AI models have training cut-offs. Anything involving current policy, legislation, guidance, or personnel is especially vulnerable to being outdated. Always check the live source.

4

Be particularly alert when the AI sounds most certain

Hallucinations are often the most confidently stated outputs. A hedged or uncertain response may be more reliable than one delivered with apparent authority.

Confidence is not a reliability signal AI models do not have access to a confidence score they can share with you. A response that begins "Research consistently shows…" or "Studies have found…" carries no more evidential weight than one that says "I'm not certain, but…". The fluency of the prose tells you nothing about its accuracy.

Discussion

Using this with students

Classroom Prompt

Ask students to use an AI tool to research a topic they already know well — a subject they've studied, a local event, a sport they follow. Ask them to find one thing the AI got wrong, one thing it oversimplified, and one thing it got right. The exercise builds critical evaluation habits far more effectively than a lecture about hallucinations.

AI systems learn from data, and data is a record of the world as it has been — not as it should be. Historical inequalities, underrepresentation, and human labelling errors are all absorbed into AI models and can be reproduced, sometimes amplified, in their outputs.

The Principle The phrase commonly used in computing is "garbage in, garbage out." If training data contains systematic bias — whether through skewed representation, flawed labelling, or historical discrimination — the model will learn and replicate it.

Types of Bias

Where does bias enter the system?

Source

Training Data Bias

The dataset over-represents particular languages, cultures, or demographics. A model trained predominantly on English-language Western internet content will perform markedly worse on other cultural contexts and may reflect those cultures inaccurately.

Source

Label Bias

When humans annotate training data — tagging images, rating responses, classifying content — their own unconscious assumptions enter the system. The model learns those human judgements as if they were objective ground truth.

Source

Representation Bias

Some groups appear far less frequently in training data, causing models to perform worse for those groups or to produce stereotyped representations of them.

Source

Algorithmic Bias

Even with a balanced dataset, the choices made in model design — what the model is optimised to predict, what counts as a "good" output — can produce systematically unfair results for particular groups.

Source

Feedback Loop Bias

AI decisions influence future data collection. If a predictive system is used to allocate resources or opportunities, its outputs reshape the real world — and the next model trains on that altered reality.

Source

Historical Bias

Data that accurately reflects historical reality may encode discrimination as if it were a neutral norm. A model trained on historical hiring decisions will learn the biases of hiring managers, not the quality of candidates.

Real-World Cases

Bias in practice

Amazon's hiring algorithm — 2018

Amazon developed an AI recruitment tool trained on a decade of the company's own hiring decisions. The system systematically downgraded CVs containing the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club" or "women's college" — and penalised graduates of all-women universities. The model had not been told to do this; it had learned that maleness was statistically correlated with being hired, and optimised accordingly. Amazon scrapped the tool in 2018 after internal audits confirmed the bias could not be corrected.

Facial recognition — differential accuracy rates

A landmark 2018 study by Joy Buolamwini and Timnit Gebru (Gender Shades) tested three major commercial facial analysis systems and found error rates of up to 34.7% for dark-skinned women, compared to under 1% for light-skinned men. The disparity was directly attributed to training datasets that contained far more white male faces than any other demographic. Several US cities subsequently banned or restricted police use of facial recognition following evidence of wrongful arrests linked to misidentification.

COMPAS — predictive sentencing in the US

COMPAS is a risk-assessment algorithm used in parts of the US criminal justice system to predict the likelihood of reoffending. A 2016 investigation by ProPublica found that the tool was nearly twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as high-risk compared to white defendants, and nearly twice as likely to falsely flag white defendants as low-risk. The tool's developers disputed some methodological points, but the case became a defining example of the high-stakes consequences of deploying biased AI in public services.

Image generation — default demographics

Until major interventions by model developers, prompting image generation tools with professional roles — "a doctor", "a lawyer", "a CEO" — reliably produced predominantly white male figures. This reflected the demographic skew of stock photography and internet images used in training. The issue is not merely aesthetic: these defaults reinforce professional stereotypes and have material consequences when such tools are used to produce educational materials, marketing, or recruitment content.

What this means for schools

AI tools used in education — for generating feedback, producing resources, or supporting assessment — carry these same biases into classrooms. Feedback generated by AI may respond differently to student names that read as particular ethnicities. Resources generated about historical periods or global cultures may reflect a Western-centric perspective by default. Staff should critically evaluate AI-produced content before sharing it, particularly when it concerns cultural representation, language register, or assessments of student work.

Questions to Ask About Any AI Tool Used in School Who collected the training data and from where? Which groups are represented in it, and which are not? Has the tool been tested for equitable performance across different student demographics? Who is accountable if the tool produces harmful outputs?

In school

Academic Integrity

Using AI to produce work that is submitted as a student's own constitutes academic misconduct under most examination board policies. But the boundaries in professional contexts are less clear — and staff who model uncritical AI use implicitly signal to students that AI-generated work is acceptable without disclosure.

In school

Transparency

When AI tools assist in creating professional materials — lesson plans, communications, reports — being open about their use is good practice. It sets a norm, helps colleagues calibrate their own use, and models the intellectual honesty we expect from students.

Principles

Responsible use in practice

AI assists — it does not decide

Professional judgement, safeguarding responsibilities, and assessment decisions cannot be delegated to an AI tool. These are responsibilities that belong to qualified professionals and must remain so.

You are responsible for what you publish

Content shared under your professional name is your responsibility, regardless of how it was produced. An AI error that passes through your hands without review is still your error professionally and reputationally.

Copyright and intellectual property

The legal status of AI-generated content remains contested. Some AI tools have been trained on copyrighted material without licence. Using AI to reproduce substantial extracts of published text, or to generate images in the distinctive style of a living artist, risks infringement. Check the terms of service of any tool before commercial or published use.

Synthetic media and deepfakes

Generative AI can produce realistic fabricated audio, video, and images of real individuals. In 2024 a Hong Kong finance worker was defrauded of £20 million after being deceived by a deepfake video call impersonating company executives. In education, the creation or distribution of synthetic media depicting real pupils or staff is a serious safeguarding matter.

Modelling good practice for students

Students do not learn AI literacy from policies. They learn it from observing how adults they respect use these tools — with scepticism, verification, and transparency, or without. How staff use AI is itself a form of teaching.

Staff Discussion Scenario

One report contains a factual error about a student's performance; another uses language a parent finds patronising. School Legal Obligation Schools are Data Controllers under UK GDPR. Any use of AI tools that involves personal data — including pupil names, SEND records, medical information, or staff HR data — must comply with the school's Data Protection Policy. The fact that a tool is convenient or widely used does not make it compliant.

Data Flow

What happens when you type into an AI tool?

What happens

Your data leaves the school network

Every message sent to a public AI tool — ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude — is transmitted to and processed on external servers operated by a US-based company. It travels across the internet and is processed outside any school or local authority data infrastructure.

What happens

Your data may be stored and logged

Most free-tier AI tools retain conversation histories by default. These logs may be reviewed by staff at the provider, used for model improvement, or retained for undefined periods. OpenAI's default settings, for example, use conversation data to train future models unless users actively opt out.

What happens

Your input could inform future model outputs

If conversation data is used in retraining, sensitive information entered by one user could — in theory — influence outputs seen by another. While this is unlikely to result in direct disclosure, it illustrates why treating free-tier AI tools as confidential is not appropriate.

What happens

US law, not UK GDPR, may apply

Once data crosses to a US-based server, it may fall under US legal jurisdiction. The US Cloud Act can compel disclosure of data held by American companies. This is materially different from the protections offered under UK GDPR and the school's standard data processing agreements.

Risk Guide

What to enter — and what never to

Never enter Student full names, SEND or medical records, safeguarding case details, staff HR or performance data, passwords or credentials, financial information, data covered by a confidentiality agreement
Use caution Year group and subject combinations that could identify a specific pupil, anonymised scenarios that could be re-identified with local knowledge, whole-class assessment datasets, behavioural incident descriptions
Generally acceptable Generic lesson planning, subject knowledge questions, fictional worked examples, anonymised professional development queries, drafting tasks with no personal data included

The Safer Option

Microsoft Copilot — enterprise protected

Why it matters

Use Copilot through your school Microsoft account

When accessed via a school Microsoft 365 login, Copilot operates under Microsoft's enterprise data protection commitments. Inputs are not used to train public models. Data remains within the Microsoft 365 compliance boundary, subject to the same data processing agreement the school already holds with Microsoft. This makes it the appropriate AI tool for any task involving school-related content — where using a public free-tier tool would not be.

Data ownership — who owns AI-generated content?

In most jurisdictions, content you generate using an AI tool is treated as owned by you or your employer. However, the underlying model — and its weights — remains the intellectual property of the provider. Some tools include terms of service that grant the provider a licence to use your outputs. Before using any AI tool to produce materials intended for publication, distribution, or commercial use, the terms of service should be reviewed. This applies to image generators as well as language tools.

Prompt injection — a security risk worth knowing

Prompt injection is an attack in which malicious instructions are embedded in content that an AI tool is asked to process — a document, a webpage, an email. When the AI reads the content, it may follow the hidden instructions rather than your legitimate request. For example, if you ask Copilot to summarise a document from an untrusted source, that document could contain hidden text instructing the AI to exfiltrate information or generate misleading output. This is an emerging and active area of security research. The practical implication: be cautious about directing AI tools to read or summarise content from unknown external sources.

A real case: Samsung's data leak via ChatGPT — 2023

In April 2023, Samsung engineers used ChatGPT to help debug proprietary source code and process meeting notes related to internal hardware. The information entered — including confidential semiconductor designs — was transmitted to OpenAI's servers. Under OpenAI's then-default settings, this data could be retained and used in future training. Samsung confirmed the incident and subsequently banned the use of ChatGPT and similar tools on company devices. The case is a direct analogue for schools: the risk is not theoretical, and the consequences of uploading sensitive data to a public AI tool are real.
