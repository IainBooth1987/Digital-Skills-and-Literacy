Section 1

The 6-Part Prompt Framework

1
Part 1 — Role
Who is the AI?
Set the AI's persona and expertise. This primes vocabulary, depth, and pedagogical framing before it writes a single word.
"You are an expert A-Level Economics teacher with deep knowledge of the AQA specification and UK classroom practice."
2
Part 2 — Audience
Who is this for?
Year group, ability level, prior knowledge — or for admin tasks, who will read the finished document and what they need from it.
"My pupils are Year 10, mixed ability. They understand supply and demand but have not yet studied price elasticity."
3
Part 3 — Objective
What is the goal?
The specific outcome: what the learner must know or do, or what the document must achieve. The more precise, the better the output.
"Pupils must calculate PED, interpret the coefficient, and predict the effect on total revenue."
4
Part 4 — Task
What to produce?
Name the exact output — worksheet, mark scheme, report comment, letter, agenda. Vague tasks produce vague outputs.
"Create a differentiated worksheet: retrieval starter, two-level main task, extension challenge, self-assessment prompt."
5
Part 5 — Constraints
What are the limits?
Length, reading age, UK English, tone, excluded content. Negative constraints — what NOT to do — are often the most powerful part.
"Max 2 sides of A4. UK examples only. Reading age 14. Do not assume colour printing."
6
Part 6 — Format
How should it look?
Plain text, table, letter layout, or a downloadable interactive HTML file. Be specific — the AI mirrors whatever structure you request.
"Plain text for Word. Pupil version first, teacher key separated. Flag uncertain content [CHECK THIS]."

Section 3

Prompt Pro Tips

1

Iterate — don't restart

Continue the conversation. "Make the starter easier", "add 3 more questions", "change the tone". Each refinement builds without losing context.

2

Be precise about length

Vague prompts produce vague outputs. "Exactly 8 questions", "no more than 2 sides of A4", "each comment 50–70 words" all produce noticeably better results.

3

Paste in spec language

Include assessment objective wording via the Spec panel. The AI mirrors the exact vocabulary pupils will encounter in exams — including command words.

4

Use "do not" instructions

"Do not use American English", "do not use US companies as examples", "do not pad with generic openers". Negative constraints are often the most powerful part of any prompt.

5

Ask for misconceptions

End nearly every resource prompt with "list the 3 most common pupil misconceptions and how to address each". High value at zero extra effort — use the Add-on field.

6

Always review before use

The prompt instructs the AI to flag uncertain content [CHECK THIS]. AI can be confidently wrong. Read the full output before sharing it with pupils or parents.
