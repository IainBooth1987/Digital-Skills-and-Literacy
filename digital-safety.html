Why this mattersPhishing is the most common route into school systems. A single click on a malicious link can trigger a ransomware attack affecting the entire network. Schools have lost weeks of data and faced significant financial costs as a result.
1
Red flag

Urgency & pressure

Phishing emails create artificial urgency to bypass your critical thinking. "Your account will be deleted in 24 hours", "Immediate action required", "Final warning".

  • Legitimate services rarely demand instant action by email
  • Urgent emails from IT, HR, or "management" are a classic vector
  • When in doubt, phone the sender directly using a known number — not the one in the email
2
Red flag

Suspicious sender address

The display name can say anything — the real email address is what matters. Hover over the sender name before clicking anything.

  • [email protected] — note the zero instead of letter o
  • [email protected] — the real domain is after the last dot
  • A spoofed colleague name is easy to fake — check the actual address
3
Red flag

Unexpected attachments

Malicious files often disguise themselves as PDFs, Word documents, or ZIP files. Opening them can install software silently.

  • Were you expecting this attachment?
  • Hover over links — does the URL match the claimed destination?
  • .exe, .zip, .docm files are particularly high risk
4
Technique

Spear phishing

Targeted attacks use your name, your school, and real context to appear convincing. More sophisticated than generic spam.

  • Uses publicly available information: school website, LinkedIn, social media
  • May reference real events, colleagues, or systems
  • Increasingly difficult to distinguish from genuine messages
5
Technique

Credential harvesting

Fake login pages that look identical to Microsoft 365, Google, or your school portal — designed to capture your username and password.

  • Always check the URL before entering credentials
  • Genuine Microsoft sign-in: login.microsoftonline.com
  • Multi-factor authentication stops stolen credentials being used
6
Response

What to do if you're unsure

Doing nothing is always safer than clicking. Reporting is always the right call.

  • Do not click any links or open any attachments
  • Forward the email to your IT team or report it via your school's process
  • If you clicked something accidentally: immediately contact IT and change your password
  • Use the "Report phishing" button in Outlook if available
The 30-second checkBefore acting on any unexpected email: (1) Check the real sender address. (2) Hover over any link and read the actual URL. (3) Ask yourself: was I expecting this? (4) If in doubt, verify by calling the sender. These four steps stop the vast majority of phishing attempts.

The password problemWeak or reused passwords are one of the leading causes of data breaches. A password breached on one site is immediately tested against email, banking, and school systems. Length beats complexity — a passphrase is both more secure and easier to remember.
1
Best practice

Use a password manager

A password manager generates and stores long, unique passwords for every site — you only remember one master password.

  • Options: Bitwarden (free), 1Password, Dashlane
  • Generates passwords like: T7#mK9&qX2vL — impossible to guess, impossible to reuse
  • Works across devices via a browser extension
  • Far more secure than writing passwords down or reusing them
2
Best practice

Strong passphrases

Three or four random words chained together are both secure and memorable — NCSC actively recommends this approach.

  • coffee-lamp-railway-bridge — 32 characters, easy to remember
  • Length matters more than special characters
  • Avoid predictable substitutions: p@ssw0rd is not secure
  • Use a different passphrase for every important account
3
Best practice

Multi-factor authentication

MFA requires a second factor — usually your phone — in addition to your password. Even if your password is stolen, MFA blocks access.

  • Enable MFA on your school Microsoft account, email, and any sensitive system
  • Use an authenticator app (Microsoft Authenticator, Google Authenticator) over SMS where possible
  • Never share MFA codes with anyone — IT will never ask for them
4
Avoid

Password mistakes to stop now

These habits are common and create significant vulnerability for both personal and school accounts.

  • Reusing the same password across sites
  • Using personal information: names, dates of birth, school name
  • Writing passwords on sticky notes near your screen
  • Sharing passwords with colleagues for shared accounts
  • Using simple sequences: Password1, Summer2024!
5
Check yourself

Have I been breached?

Visit haveibeenpwned.com to check whether your email address appears in any known data breach — updated in real time.

  • Enter your school and personal email addresses
  • If breached: change the password on that site immediately
  • Check whether you used the same password anywhere else — change those too
6
School context

Shared account risks

Shared login accounts make breach investigation impossible — you cannot determine who accessed what when.

  • Every staff member should have their own named account
  • Shared resource accounts should use a documented process — not a sticky note password
  • When a staff member leaves: their access should be revoked immediately, not "when we get round to it"

Your legal responsibilityUK GDPR applies to every member of staff who handles personal data — not just the DPO. Ignorance is not a defence. A personal data breach must be reported to the ICO within 72 hours if it is likely to result in a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms.
1
Definition

What counts as personal data

Personal data is any information that identifies or could identify a living person — directly or in combination with other data.

  • Name, address, date of birth, photo, email address
  • IP address, device identifiers, location data
  • School performance data, attendance records
  • Special category data: health, SEND, ethnicity, religion — has stronger protections
2
Principle

Data minimisation

Only collect, use, and store the minimum personal data needed for the specific purpose. If you don't need it, don't hold it.

  • Don't collect data "just in case" it might be useful
  • Delete data you no longer need in line with your retention schedule
  • Don't share data between systems without a lawful basis
3
Risk area

AI tools & personal data

Entering pupil names, SEND data, or identifiable information into AI tools such as ChatGPT or Copilot (personal account) is a likely GDPR breach — that data leaves your control.

  • Use [Pupil] as a placeholder — always
  • Your school Microsoft 365 Copilot has enterprise protection — free tools do not
  • The DfE's own guidance explicitly names this as a data protection risk area
4
Risk area

Email mistakes

Sending an email to the wrong person is one of the most frequently reported data breaches in schools — and one of the most avoidable.

  • Double-check recipient addresses before sending anything sensitive
  • Use BCC when emailing a group of parents (not CC)
  • Be careful with Reply All — check who is in the thread
  • Don't send personal data via unencrypted email to external addresses
5
Process

If a breach occurs

Speed matters — you have 72 hours to report to the ICO if the breach is likely to cause risk to individuals.

  • Report it to your DPO immediately — do not wait to assess it yourself
  • Document what happened, what data was involved, who was affected
  • The DPO will assess whether ICO notification is required
  • Never try to cover up a breach — late reporting is a separate offence
6
Your rights

Subject access requests

Any individual — including a pupil or parent — can request a copy of all personal data the school holds about them. Schools must respond within one month.

  • SARs must be fulfilled free of charge
  • You cannot withhold data because it is inconvenient
  • Third-party data (e.g. other pupils' names) must be redacted before disclosure
  • Log all SARs — your DPO should manage the process

Your professional digital identityAs a teacher or school leader, your online presence is visible to pupils, parents, governors, and prospective employers. Content you post — or that others tag you in — can surface years later. Understanding your footprint is part of professional responsibility.
1
Awareness

What is your footprint?

Your digital footprint is everything that can be found about you online — active posts you've made and passive data collected without your knowledge.

  • Social media posts, profile photos, check-ins, and tagged content
  • Comments on news articles or forums under a recognisable name
  • Professional profiles: LinkedIn, school website, conference talks
  • Data held by companies: purchases, search history, location data
2
Privacy settings

Audit your social media

Regularly review the privacy settings on every platform you use — defaults often favour visibility over privacy.

  • Set personal accounts to private or friends-only
  • Review what a non-follower can see — this is what a pupil or parent sees
  • Untag yourself from posts or photos that are professionally inappropriate
  • Consider whether a public LinkedIn is sufficient for professional presence
3
Professional boundary

Staff–pupil boundaries online

Accepting pupil follow requests on personal social media — even once they have left school — carries professional risk and blurs important boundaries.

  • Do not accept current pupils as social media followers or friends
  • Be cautious about accepting recent leavers — professional conduct codes still apply
  • Never communicate with pupils through personal social media or messaging apps
  • Use school-sanctioned platforms only for pupil communication
4
Good practice

Separating professional & personal

Maintain a clear separation between your professional digital presence and personal accounts — it protects you and your school.

  • Use your school email exclusively for school business
  • Do not use personal cloud storage (Dropbox, personal Google Drive) for school data
  • Think before posting anything during school trips or events — photos of pupils require consent
  • Google yourself periodically to see what is publicly visible
5
Risk

Photographs & data protection

Photographs of pupils are personal data under UK GDPR. Capturing or sharing pupil images has clear rules you must follow.

  • Only photograph pupils if your school has parental consent on file
  • Never post photos of identifiable pupils on personal social media
  • School events shared on official school channels must respect opted-out pupils
  • Delete photographs from personal devices after transferring to school systems
6
AI & footprint

AI-generated content about you

AI tools can scrape publicly available information and generate summaries, images, or quotes that appear authentic but may be inaccurate or harmful.

  • Audit what is publicly available about you — reduce it where possible
  • Be aware deepfake audio and video of real people is increasingly accessible
  • Report any AI-generated content misrepresenting you to your school leadership
Safeguarding responsibilityUnder Keeping Children Safe in Education 2025, all staff have a duty to report concerns about a child's online safety. You do not need to be certain — if something doesn't feel right, report it to your DSL immediately. The online world is not a separate space; risks there are as real as any offline safeguarding concern.

Online risks often present through changes in pupil behaviour rather than direct disclosure. Signs that a pupil may be experiencing online harm include: becoming withdrawn after using devices, distress when phones are taken away, secretive behaviour about what they are viewing or who they are talking to, or unexplained gifts or money.

Online risks you may encounter as a professional:

  • Cyberbullying between pupils — including outside school hours, which can still create a duty of care
  • Grooming — adults building inappropriate relationships with pupils online
  • Radicalisation — extremist content or contact through gaming platforms, social media, or messaging apps
  • Sexting or sharing of intimate images — increasingly common, never treat as trivial
  • Exposure to harmful, age-inappropriate, or illegal content

Every member of staff has a legal duty to act on safeguarding concerns. You do not need proof. You do not need to investigate. You need to report.

The process:

  • Report any concern to your Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) immediately — the same day, the same lesson if necessary
  • Do not ask leading questions or attempt to investigate yourself
  • Do not promise a pupil confidentiality — explain that you have a duty to share with people who can help
  • Make a factual written record of what the pupil said, in their own words, as soon as possible
  • Out of hours: follow your school's emergency contacts procedure

Under the DfE's digital and technology standards, schools are required to have appropriate filtering and monitoring of internet access. As of 2023 this includes specific requirements for schools to review systems annually.

What this means for staff:

  • Filtering blocks access to harmful categories of content on school networks and devices
  • Monitoring alerts designated staff when pupils attempt to access or search for harmful content
  • This is not surveillance of staff — it applies to all users on school networks including pupils
  • If you discover a pupil has found a way around filtering, report it to your IT team immediately
  • Pupils using personal mobile data can bypass school filtering — this is a known gap

AI tools present new and rapidly evolving child safety risks. The DfE's generative AI in education guidance requires schools to carefully assess pupil-facing use of AI tools for compliance with safeguarding duties.

Key concerns:

  • AI chatbots can generate harmful, age-inappropriate, or inaccurate content if not appropriately restricted
  • Pupils may use AI to produce fake communications — e.g. phishing emails appearing to come from school
  • AI image generation tools can produce harmful imagery — including of minors — if unfiltered
  • AI tools may collect pupil data in ways that breach GDPR if the tool has not been approved by your school
  • Before approving any AI tool for pupil use, check the DfE's Generative AI Product Safety Standards

Digital wellbeing matters for staff tooThe research on digital technology and wellbeing applies as much to teaching professionals as to pupils. Constant connectivity, out-of-hours email, and the pressure to be perpetually responsive can significantly affect sleep, attention, and mental health. Managing your relationship with technology is a professional skill.
1
Boundary

After-hours email

There is no professional obligation to respond to emails outside working hours. Research consistently links constant email monitoring to elevated stress and disrupted sleep.

  • Use Outlook's Schedule Send to write emails at any time without creating pressure for immediate response
  • Agree a team norm around expected response times — it protects everyone
  • Turn off email push notifications outside working hours on your personal device
  • The school has no legal right to require instant response at 9pm
2
Boundary

Managing device time

Intentional use — knowing why you are picking up your phone — is more protective of wellbeing than abstinence. Build in deliberate non-screen time.

  • Use Screen Time (iOS) or Digital Wellbeing (Android) to audit actual usage
  • Charge your phone outside the bedroom — protects sleep significantly
  • Build at least one sustained offline period into each day
  • Meetings without devices improve engagement and recall
3
Awareness

Doom scrolling & the attention economy

Social media and news platforms are designed to maximise time on site — often through negative emotional content. This is by design, not accident.

  • The algorithm prioritises outrage and anxiety — it keeps you scrolling
  • Distinguish between "informed" and "endlessly refreshing" — they feel similar but are different
  • Curate your feeds: mute, unfollow, use time limits
  • News apps can be replaced with a once-daily scheduled read
4
Modelling

Visible choices in the classroom

How staff use technology in front of pupils sends a clear message about what professional, intentional device use looks like.

  • Be intentional about when devices are used in class — model purposeful use
  • Demonstrate that it is normal to put a device away during conversations
  • Discuss your own digital choices with pupils where appropriate — it normalises reflection
  • Acknowledging the challenges of attention in a digital world is not weakness
Practical strategies for a healthier digital relationship
1

The two-hour evening rule

Avoid bright screens in the two hours before sleep. Blue light suppresses melatonin production and delays sleep onset. Night mode helps but does not eliminate the effect entirely. Reading, non-screen conversation, or light physical activity in the evening improves sleep quality measurably.

2

Single-tasking and deep work

Multitasking between screens, applications, and notifications significantly reduces cognitive performance — research suggests by as much as 40%. Block time in your calendar for tasks requiring sustained attention. Close email, silence notifications, and put your phone face down. Twenty uninterrupted minutes of focused work outperforms an hour of fragmented attention.

3

Regular digital audits

Once a term, review the apps and notifications on your devices. Delete anything you have not opened in three months. Turn off all non-essential push notifications — the world does not end when you stop receiving them in real time. Each notification is a small but real interruption to your attention.

4

If you are concerned about your digital habits

Concerns about your own relationship with technology — or a colleague's — are as legitimate as any other wellbeing concern. Speak to your line manager or access your school's employee assistance programme. Digital burnout is real and recognised — it does not require a crisis to seek support.
