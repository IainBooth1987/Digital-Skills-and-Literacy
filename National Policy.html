Six key documents

Official Guidance & Legislation

DfE Policy Paper Department for Education Policy
Generative Artificial Intelligence in Education
Updated: August 2025  ·  Applies to: England
The DfE's core position on generative AI use in schools and colleges. Sets out the government's expectations for safe and effective use by staff and pupils, the known risks, and the principles schools must apply when making decisions about AI tools.
  • Safety must be the top priority when deciding whether to use any generative AI tool in an education setting
  • Schools choosing to use pupil-facing AI must ensure compliance with data protection law and Keeping Children Safe in Education
  • The quality and accuracy of any AI-generated content remains the professional responsibility of the staff member who produced it
  • Risks include hallucination, bias, intellectual property concerns, and data protection breaches
  • AI-generated content could include realistic phishing emails or harmful material — risk assessments must address unauthorised use cases
Last updated August 2025

DfE Guidance Toolkit Department for Education Guidance
Data Protection in Schools
Updated: October 2025  ·  Applies to: Maintained schools, academies
A comprehensive DfE toolkit covering all aspects of UK GDPR compliance in schools — from lawful bases for data processing to managing breaches, subject access requests, and the Data Use and Access Act 2025. Includes dedicated guidance on generative AI and data protection.
  • Schools must have a lawful basis for every category of data they process — this includes data processed by third-party AI tools
  • Special category data (health, SEND, ethnicity, religion) requires an additional condition beyond the standard lawful basis
  • Data breaches likely to cause risk to individuals must be reported to the ICO within 72 hours
  • Subject access requests must be fulfilled free of charge within one calendar month
  • Specific section on generative AI risks: using free AI tools with pupil data is likely to constitute a data breach
Last updated October 2025

DfE Statutory Guidance Department for Education Statutory
Keeping Children Safe in Education 2025
Updated: September 2025  ·  Applies to: All schools and colleges, England
The statutory safeguarding guidance that every member of staff in a school or college must read Part 1 of annually. Sets out the legal duties on schools to safeguard and promote the welfare of children under 18, including online safety and safer recruitment. Compliance is mandatory — this is law, not guidance.
  • Every member of staff has a responsibility to act on any safeguarding concern — this includes online safety concerns
  • Online safety is explicitly named as a safeguarding issue — risks include grooming, radicalisation, cyberbullying, and sexting
  • Schools must have robust filtering and monitoring of internet use by pupils
  • Staff must not communicate with pupils via personal social media or messaging apps
  • The Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) must be accessible to all staff — including for online concerns
September 2025 — all staff must read Part 1

DfE Guidance Department for Education Standards
Generative AI: Product Safety Standards
Updated: January 2026  ·  Applies to: England (edtech suppliers + schools)
Standards for the capabilities and safety features that generative AI products must meet to be considered safe for use in educational settings. Primarily directed at edtech developers, but schools should use it to assess whether any AI tool they are considering deploying meets the required standards — particularly for pupil-facing use.
  • AI products used in schools should prevent generation of harmful, age-inappropriate, or extremist content
  • 2026 update added new standards on cognitive development, emotional and social development, mental health, and manipulation
  • Products should be transparent about when a user is interacting with AI, not a human
  • Schools can use these standards as a checklist when evaluating any AI tool before procurement or deployment
  • Pupil-facing AI tools must be assessed against these standards before being approved for use
Updated January 2026 — includes new wellbeing standards

DfE Digital Standards Department for Education Standards
Cyber Security — Core Standard for Schools
Updated: February 2026  ·  Applies to: All schools and colleges, England
One of the DfE's six core digital and technology standards for schools and colleges. Schools are expected to be working towards this standard by 2030. Covers cyber risk assessment, account security, patching, network protection, and incident response. Cyber attacks have caused school closures, data loss, and significant financial cost.
  • Schools must conduct an annual cyber risk assessment and review every term
  • Multi-factor authentication must be applied to all staff accounts, especially email and admin systems
  • User accounts must be reviewed regularly — access must be removed immediately when staff leave
  • Schools should work towards Cyber Essentials certification (mandatory for colleges, recommended for schools)
  • An incident response plan must exist and be tested — staff should know their role in the event of a cyber attack
Updated February 2026 — target year 2030

NCSC Guidance National Cyber Security Centre Technical Guidance
Machine Learning Principles
Published by NCSC  ·  Applies to: All organisations using ML/AI
The NCSC's technical guidance on the security considerations of using machine learning and AI systems. Explains how ML models can be attacked, manipulated, or behave unexpectedly — and what organisations should consider when procuring, deploying, or interacting with AI tools. Relevant background reading for school leaders making AI procurement decisions.
  • AI systems can be manipulated through "prompt injection" — crafted inputs that cause the system to behave in unintended ways
  • Training data can be poisoned, causing the model to produce systematically biased or harmful outputs
  • AI systems may behave differently in deployment than in testing — ongoing monitoring is essential
  • Before deploying any AI tool, organisations should understand what data it was trained on and how it is updated
  • The principles of secure software procurement apply to AI tools: vet suppliers, review data handling, test before deploying with pupils
NCSC — the UK's National Cyber Security Centre
